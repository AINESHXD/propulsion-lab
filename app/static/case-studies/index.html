Ten engines that shaped the jet age

Short, original write-ups of landmark turbofans — the architecture, the cycle, and why each one mattered. Read in order they trace the arc from the loud low-bypass workhorses of the 1960s to today's high-bypass, composite-fan, geared and three-spool designs.

Pratt & Whitney · 1964Pratt & Whitney JT8DLow-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 1.0 – 1.7The engine that made the short- and medium-haul jetliner economic. A rugged low-bypass turbofan, the JT8D was for two decades the most common commercial jet engine in the Western world. General Electric · 1971General Electric CF6High-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 4.3 – 5.3The engine that took GE from the military into the heart of the widebody market. Derived from the TF39 that powered the C-5 Galaxy, the CF6 spent five decades under the wings of nearly every long-haul twin and tri-jet. Pratt & Whitney · 1987Pratt & Whitney PW4000High-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 4.8 – 6.4Pratt & Whitney's widebody mainstay of the 1990s. Built in three fan diameters from one design philosophy, the PW4000 succeeded the JT9D and powered almost every long-haul airframe of its era. International Aero Engines · 1989IAE V2500High-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 4.6A clean, efficient high-bypass turbofan built by a five-nation consortium — and for three decades the A320 family's other engine choice alongside the CFM56. CFM International (GE + Safran) · 1996CFM56-5B / -7BHigh-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 5.0 – 5.5The best-selling jet engine in history. The CFM56 powered the narrowbody market for three decades and proved the GE-Safran partnership that went on to build the LEAP. General Electric · 1995General Electric GE90High-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 8 – 9The most powerful jet engine ever flown, and the one that made composite fan blades mainstream. Built exclusively for the Boeing 777, the GE90 set a thrust record that still stands. General Electric · 2011General Electric GEnxHigh-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 8 – 9.6The GE90's technology distilled into a lighter, cleaner engine for the 787 and 747-8. The GEnx carried composites further, added a low-emissions combustor, and dropped engine bleed air entirely on the Dreamliner. CFM International (GE + Safran) · 2016CFM LEAP-1A / -1BHigh-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 9 – 11The CFM56's successor and a showcase of aerospace materials: woven carbon-fibre fan blades, ceramic-matrix composites in the hot section, and roughly 15% better fuel burn. Pratt & Whitney · 2016Pratt & Whitney GTF (PW1000G)Geared high-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 9 – 12The defining efficiency idea of the 2010s narrowbody generation: a reduction gearbox that lets the fan turn slowly and the low-pressure spool turn fast, each near its own aerodynamic optimum. Rolls-Royce · 2015Rolls-Royce Trent XWBHigh-bypass turbofan · BPR ≈ 9.3Rolls-Royce's three-spool answer for the Airbus A350. At entry the most efficient large civil engine flying, the Trent XWB showcases an architecture no other manufacturer uses at this scale.
